### ✅ Task C2.3 — Write Judge Q&A Answer Sheet
**Time: 1.5 hours**

Create `05_QA_Prep/judge_qa_answers.md`. Write a clear, prepared answer for each question. Keep each answer to 3–4 sentences max.

---

**Q1: "How accurate is your model?"**
> *"On held-out 2023–2024 data our model achieves [X]% accuracy for 30-day predictions — Member A will give you the exact number on Day 3. Accuracy drops to approximately 70–75% for 90-day predictions, which is why we show confidence intervals rather than a single number. We believe honest uncertainty is more useful than false precision."*

---

**Q2: "How is this different from existing drought indices like SPI or PDSI?"**
> *"Standard drought indices like SPI are retrospective — they tell you how dry it was last month. AquaSentry is forward-looking — it predicts how dry it will be 30 to 90 days from now, at the district level, with confidence intervals. No existing tool does this specifically for the Amu Darya and Syr Darya basins."*

---

**Q3: "Kazhydromet already does weather forecasting. Why is yours better?"**
> *"Kazhydromet forecasts national-level weather — temperature and rainfall probabilities. AquaSentry forecasts water stress — a fundamentally different output that integrates rainfall, temperature, evapotranspiration, historical river flow, and glacial melt into a single district-level risk score. We complement Kazhydromet; we don't compete with it."*

---

**Q4: "What happens with cloud cover blocking your satellite data?"**
> *"CHIRPS, our primary rainfall dataset, already incorporates a cloud-masking algorithm and gap-fills using ground station data. For persistent cloud cover, our system flags those districts with a lower confidence score in the output rather than suppressing the prediction entirely."*

---

**Q5: "What's the cost to operate this system?"**
> *"Near zero. All data sources are free — NASA CHIRPS, ERA5 climate data, and Copernicus satellite imagery are publicly available at no cost. Compute costs for running the model and hosting the dashboard are under $50 per month on commodity cloud infrastructure."*

---

**Q6: "Who pays for this? What's the business model?"**
> *"Three viable paths: first, government licensing — water ministries in Kazakhstan and Uzbekistan have direct budget for decision-support tools. Second, international development grants — FAO, UNDP, and ADB all fund water security initiatives in Central Asia. Third, ESG data services — carbon credit and impact investors need granular water risk data for their Central Asian portfolio companies."*

---

**Q7: "Is this scalable beyond Kazakhstan?"**
> *"Completely — the architecture is region-agnostic. The same satellite data and model pipeline work for the Nile Basin, the Indus Basin, and the Euphrates-Tigris system. Anywhere transboundary water scarcity is a political and humanitarian issue, AquaSentry applies directly."*

---

**Q8: "What about the Qosh-Tepa Canal and upstream diversion?"**
> *"This is exactly why AquaSentry is critical. The Qosh-Tepa Canal in Afghanistan diverts [X km³/year] of Amu Darya water upstream, directly reducing flows into Kazakhstan and Uzbekistan. Our model can simulate this diversion scenario — enter a reduced river flow assumption, and the map immediately shows which districts face the highest impact. We can model geopolitical water risk, not just climate risk."*
